The complete guide to your dog's
annual wellness routine

From nutrition checks to coat care, our veterinary team walks you through everything a modern dog owner needs to know about keeping their companion in peak health year-round.

Dr. Sarah Chen Lead Veterinarian · Petcare Spa

Every dog owner wants the same thing: a happy, healthy companion who thrives well into old age. Yet for most families, pet wellness care is reactive rather than proactive — a visit to the vet when something goes wrong, rather than a structured annual routine designed to prevent problems before they start.

After more than a decade working with thousands of dogs and their owners, our veterinary team at Petcare Spa has distilled what genuinely matters — and what can safely be deprioritised. This guide is the result of that accumulated experience.

Why an annual wellness routine matters

Dogs age faster than we do. A single year in their life represents roughly seven human years in terms of physiological change. This means a health issue that develops slowly over twelve months can appear to emerge almost suddenly when measured against a human timescale.

Annual wellness checks create a longitudinal record of your dog's health. Weight trends, organ function markers, dental progression, coat condition — all of these are far more meaningful when measured over time than at a single point. Vets who see your dog every year don't just treat problems; they recognise deviations from your dog's normal, which is the most powerful diagnostic tool there is.

"The dogs that age the best aren't the ones we treat when they're sick — they're the ones whose owners invest in keeping them well."
Dr. Sarah Chen, Lead Veterinarian

The core wellness assessment

A comprehensive annual check should cover more than vaccinations and a quick listen to the heart. At Petcare Spa, our full wellness assessment includes the following components:

Physical examination

A thorough nose-to-tail examination remains the single most valuable diagnostic tool in veterinary medicine. A skilled vet can identify lumps, skin changes, joint stiffness, dental disease, eye abnormalities, and early signs of dozens of conditions — all without a single test. Budget at least 30 minutes for this examination; anything shorter is likely incomplete.

Blood panel and urinalysis

Annual bloodwork is particularly important for dogs over five years old, but we recommend beginning it at three for larger breeds, who age faster. A baseline panel typically includes:

  • Complete blood count (CBC) to assess red and white blood cells
  • Comprehensive metabolic panel covering liver and kidney function
  • Thyroid hormone levels, especially in middle-aged and older dogs
  • Urinalysis to screen for kidney disease, diabetes, and infection

These results mean very little in isolation. Their value lies in the trend — which is why consistent annual testing at the same laboratory, using the same reference ranges, matters so much.

Regular outdoor activity is one of the most evidence-backed contributors to long-term canine health.

Nutrition: the most undervalued pillar

We see hundreds of dogs each year whose health issues have a nutritional root cause. Obesity alone is the most common preventable condition in domestic dogs — and unlike genetic conditions, it is entirely within the owner's control.

The annual wellness appointment is the ideal time to reassess your dog's diet. Nutritional needs change significantly with age, activity level, reproductive status, and health conditions. A food that was appropriate at age two may be actively harmful at age nine.

Specific questions worth exploring with your vet each year:

  • Is my dog's body condition score appropriate for their breed and age?
  • Are there breed-specific nutritional considerations I should be aware of?
  • Should I be supplementing for joint health, coat condition, or cognitive function?
  • How does my dog's activity level this year compare to last year, and does the food reflect that?

Dental health: the silent epidemic

By age three, approximately 80% of dogs show some degree of dental disease. By age five, many have progressed to conditions that cause chronic pain, tooth loss, and — most dangerously — bacterial seeding into the bloodstream that can affect heart and kidney function.

Despite this, dental care remains the most consistently neglected area of routine pet wellness. Annual professional cleaning under anaesthesia is not a luxury; for most adult dogs, it is a medical necessity. Daily tooth brushing at home significantly slows the progression between professional cleanings.

"Most owners are surprised to learn that dental disease is the single most common reason for premature organ failure in otherwise healthy dogs over age seven."
Dr. James Park, Senior Veterinarian

Coat and skin: your dog's external health report

The condition of a dog's coat is one of the most reliable indicators of internal health. A dull, dry, or thinning coat can signal nutritional deficiency, thyroid dysfunction, allergies, or hormonal imbalance — all of which annual bloodwork helps to identify.

Professional grooming, done on a schedule appropriate to the breed, serves a medical purpose beyond aesthetics. Regular grooming allows for early detection of skin masses, parasites, ear infections, and interdigital cysts. Our grooming team documents any skin changes they observe and flags them to our veterinary staff directly.

Looking ahead: senior wellness planning

For dogs seven and older, we recommend moving to twice-yearly check-ups. The rate of change accelerates in senior years, and six months can be a meaningful interval in terms of disease progression.

At Petcare Spa, we offer a dedicated Senior Wellness Plan that includes biannual blood panels, a joint mobility assessment, cognitive function screening, and dietary consultation. Early detection of conditions like arthritis, kidney disease, and canine cognitive dysfunction allows for interventions that meaningfully improve quality and length of life.

The goal of every annual check is not just to confirm that your dog is healthy today — it is to give them the best possible chance of being healthy tomorrow.
